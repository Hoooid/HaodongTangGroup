# Paper Post Editorial Guide

This document records editorial rules for future paper announcements and public-facing paper highlights on `nanophotonics.cc`. It is an internal writing guide and should not be pasted into article bodies.

## Chinese Paper Posts

Use a lightly celebratory academic public-account style when the post announces a group paper.

Preferred structure:

1. Glory moment: announce the paper, journal, topic, and why it matters for the group or field.
2. Core analysis: explain the technical contribution with figures and short, readable sections.
3. Team and paper information: list authors, corresponding authors, affiliations, DOI, and original article link.

Write with confidence, but keep claims tied to the paper. Avoid unsupported phrases such as "first", "best", or "breakthrough" unless the paper or official source supports them directly.

## English Paper Posts

Keep the English version more restrained and professional. It can still be clear and readable, but should sound like a scholarly website highlight rather than a promotional announcement.

## Metrics

Do not treat metrics as a leaderboard. Translate each metric into an imaging consequence that readers can perceive:

- Dark current and noise: whether the dark frame is clean and whether weak light is visible.
- Responsivity and EQE: whether faint signals become measurable without excessive noise or bias.
- Response speed: whether moving targets blur or whether high-frame-rate capture is possible.
- Linear dynamic range: whether bright and dark details can be retained in the same scene.

Use performance numbers only with proper context, such as wavelength, bias, device structure, or measurement condition when available.

## Visuals

Use authorized original paper figures when possible. For review articles, the paper's roadmap, metric, strategy, and system-integration figures should form the backbone of the post. Redraw figures only when original figures cannot be reused cleanly or when the user explicitly asks for a new explanatory graphic.

## Article Boundary

Keep editorial guidance out of published articles. The article should speak to readers about the work itself, not explain how future posts should be written.
